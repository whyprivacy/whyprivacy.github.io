Why Privacy?

If you have nothing to hide, no one needs to snoop

My reasons for privacy are both logical and emotional. Software and hardware are susceptible to vulnerabilities whether it's from an intentional back-door, poor security design, or even a small bug in a software giant's code.

This page serves as a reference for myself as well as an easy way for me to provide some insight into everything I've been reading and hearing lately through The Privacy, Security, & OSINT Podcast.

Popular Services With Privacy Issues

Google
Collects your data from all Google services including email, drive, calendar, and photos, as well as Google searches and the websites you visit (especially if using Chrome), and uses it for advertising, since it's an ad company

A bug in Google+ gave outside developers access to private profile data between 2015 and March 2018
Google failed to report the incident among fears of reputational damage and triggering "immediate regulatory interest"
Microsoft
Microsoft tracks data such as the type of hardware you use, which applications you have installed, some usage details (e.g. how often you launch or use the applications) and crash information on your devices.
Some of this can be turned off in settings, but it's all on by default
Office 365 collects data about the use of its products, like the sentences before and after the words you look up or retype
Facebook
Collects all data explicitely given to Facebook and their partners, as well as implied interests through interaction with the services. Uses this for advertising and has proven not to vet who it shares data with (Cambridge Analytica)
Instagram
"We do not claim ownership of your content, but you grant us a license to use it."
This means Instagram can sublicense your photos to any company for a fee without paying you.

Because you still own the content you post, "you agree to pay for all royalties, fees, and any other monies owed by reason of Content you post"
This means if Instagram uses your photo then gets sued for not having the rights, you get to pay
Tinder
Tinder requires access to your Facebook profile, including photos, location, statuses, etc., and "by choosing to provide this information, you consent to" their "processing of that information"
They also collect device info like IP adress, device ID and type, Google and Apple advertising IDs, info on your wireless and mobile network like service provider, input from accelerometers, gyroscopes, and compasses, and "precise geolocation may occur in the background even when you arenâ€™t using the services"
Venmo
Users give info about each transaction which is public by default, allowing anyone to form entire transaction profiles on users by using their API
Uber
Has suffered multiple breaches, failed to report a new breach that occured while under investigation by the FTC for a breach from two years earlier. Paid the hackers $100,000 to keep quiet about it and "delete the data"

Services I Use

ProtonMail
End-to-end encrypted email that even ProtonMail can't read
Premium adds alias addresses that forward to your main inbox
Your password aids in decrypting your inbox, so old emails become unreadable if you change your password
Blur
Masked email, phone number, and credit cards* (small fee per purchase)
Browser extension blocks trackers and ads
Privacy
Create free unique virtual debit cards linked to the first vendor they're used with
Can set a monthly limit for subscriptions or make it a single-use card
Private Internet Access
Swiss-based VPN that hides your IP address and encrypts all internet traffic, so even your ISP can't see what you do online

Lastpass
Free encrypted cloud-based password manager
Malwarebytes
Free PC utility that reactively scans for and removes malware, ransomeware, adware, and more
Can scan full system, folder, or file
Premium includes proactive, real-time protection
Why should you care?
Firefox
Browser that's not made by an ad company, so it doesn't track everything you do
Still must be careful with extensions and
NoScript
Free and open-source Firefox browser extension that blocks all scripts on websites
Enable only the essential scripts from trusted sources for the page to work
Leave all the annoying ad and tracking scripts disabled
uBlock Origin
Free and open-source browser extension that blocks ads in basic mode, functions like NoScript in advanced mode

Ad blocking ≠ theft when ads cost you half your mobile data
Ads can also be malicious, loaded with viruses, spyware, malware, and can even mine cryptocurrencies using your computer
